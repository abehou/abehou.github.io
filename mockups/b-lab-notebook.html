Abe Hou / notebook

// phd student, stanford cs // 2026-04-22

I study language models where the answers aren’t checkable. Three open questions drive my work: can LLMs reason in non-verifiable domains? what determines human-AI collaboration? can simulated societies tell us anything real?

At Stanford I work with Diyi Yang (primary), and collaborate with Chris Manning and Dan Ho. Undergrad at Johns Hopkins with Ben Van Durme, Daniel Khashabi, and Tianxing He.

/publications

├── non-verifiable domains

#legal-reasoning

Gaps or Hallucinations? Scrutinizing Machine-Generated Legal Analysis

Hou, Jurayj, Holzenberger, Blair-Stanek, Van Durme
EMNLP 2025 NLLP Workshop
Legal analysis generation fails in ways standard text metrics miss. We define gaps — structural differences from human-written analysis that aren’t strict errors — and train a detector that achieves 67% F1.
pdfcode
#retrieval

CLERC: A Dataset for Legal Case Retrieval and RAG

Hou, Weller, Qin, Yang, Lawrie, Holzenberger, Blair-Stanek, Van Durme
NAACL 2025 Findings
A legal IR + RAG benchmark built with practicing lawyers. GPT-4o generates the highest-ROUGE analyses and also hallucinates the most.
pdfcode

├── social simulation

#policy

Can A Society of Generative Agents Simulate Human Behavior and Inform Public Health Policy?

Hou, Du, Wang, Zhang, Wang, Liang, Khashabi, Gardner, He
COLM 2025
100 LLM-powered agents on a social network, calibrated against census demographics. We test vaccine-hesitancy interventions and find both promising signal and real alignment failures.
