Stanford CS, working with Diyi Yang, Chris Manning, and Dan Ho.

The easy problems in machine learning are the ones with verifiers: compile-or-not, win-or-lose, pass-or-fail. Everything interesting happens outside that regime. A lawyer doesn’t get a unit test for a brief. A public-health officer can’t A/B test hesitancy interventions on a real population. A software team collaborating with an AI tool has no ground-truth trajectory for what good collaboration looks like. These are the problems I care about.

a sketch: information propagation through a small agent society

Three threads run through the work. The first is reasoning in non-verifiable domains — mostly through the lens of law, where a good answer has to cite precedent, track counterfactuals, and hold up to adversarial reading. Much of my undergrad at Johns Hopkins with Ben Van Durme went into building datasets and evaluations that don’t collapse into ROUGE scores.

The second is social simulation. I’m drawn to the oldest question in applied math: when does a simulation tell you something real about the thing it’s modeling, and when is it an elaborate cartoon? With Lauren Gardner and Tianxing He we built VacSim, a hundred-agent society calibrated on census data, to ask whether LLM-powered agents can stand in for real people when modeling policy effects. The answer is partially, and the failures are more interesting than the successes.

Can a Society of Generative Agents Simulate Human Behavior and Inform Public Health Policy? Hou et al. — COLM 2025

The third is human–AI collaboration, which is new territory for me at Stanford with Diyi Yang. We’re running a longitudinal study using a controlled programming task to measure what actually changes when developers work with AI tools — not "is it faster," but what shifts in how people reason, debug, and delegate. A paper is in progress.

Before all this, I worked on watermarking machine-generated text with Yulia Tsvetkov and Tianxing He at the University of Washington. That line of work taught me a lot about what I don’t want to do long-term, which is its own kind of useful.
